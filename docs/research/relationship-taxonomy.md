# Relationship taxonomy for Mend

Research brief. Written 2026-07-19. All web sources were viewed on 2026-07-19
unless a different date is noted next to the link.

**Scope.** Mend now serves all committed romantic relationships, not only
marriages. This document is the working taxonomy: who the couples are, what
actually strains them, where generic marriage advice misfires, and the one
intervention worth building first for each.

**Evidence honesty.** Where a claim has a real citation it has a URL. Where the
literature is thin or contested, it says so in plain words. Nothing in this file
is invented. Several popular relationship statistics circulating online come
from content farms with no traceable source, and those were excluded on purpose.
One well-known study on illness and divorce is discussed below precisely because
it was retracted, and Mend should never quote it.

---

## 1. The single most important structural finding

**These categories are not a list to pick one from. They are overlapping tags.**

A real user can be, at once: queer, long distance, neurodivergent, in recovery,
and co-parenting a stepchild. A single-select "what kind of couple are you"
onboarding question will be wrong for a large share of users and will feel
reductive to exactly the people who most need to feel seen.

Recommended model: six independent axes, multi-select, all optional, all
skippable, all editable later.

| Axis | Values (multi-select) |
| --- | --- |
| Stage | dating seriously, engaged, newly living together, newlywed, together many years, empty nest, reconciling after a breakup, rebuilding after a separation |
| Household | living together, living apart by choice, long distance, separated by work, separated by immigration or deployment |
| Structure | monogamous, consensually non-monogamous, legally married, legally partnered, not legally partnered, remarried or repartnered |
| Family | no children, no children by choice, young children, teens, adult children, stepchildren, co-parenting with an ex, caregiving for a parent, caregiving for each other |
| Identity and context | queer, interracial, intercultural, interfaith, one partner not out or partly out, significant age gap, military or first responder, immigrant or mixed status |
| Health and mind | ADHD, autistic, other neurodivergence, chronic illness, disability, mental health condition, in recovery from addiction, supporting a partner in recovery |

Design consequences:

1. Content selection should be **additive filtering**, not routing. Show the
   base exercise, then layer 1 to 2 context notes from the tags that apply.
   Do not build 22 separate parallel journeys. That is unmaintainable and it
   fragments the product.
2. Every tag must be **optional and reversible**. Tags such as "queer",
   "not out", "immigration status", and "in recovery" are sensitive. They should
   be stored locally first, never surfaced to the partner without explicit
   consent, and never used in a shared or exportable summary by default.
3. **Outness asymmetry is a first-class case.** If one partner is out and the
   other is not, a shared couple space can create real-world risk. Any feature
   that syncs answers between two phones needs a per-item privacy control, not
   just a global one.
4. The safety layer is **global and never conditional on tags**. The domestic
   violence gate, the "this is an educational tool, not therapy" framing, and the
   crisis resources apply to every relationship type. Several types below have
   an *additional* red flag, layered on top, never in place of.

## 2. What generic marriage advice gets wrong across the board

Five failure modes recur in almost every category. Naming them here so each
entry below does not have to repeat them.

1. **Assumed shared address.** Most exercises assume nightly proximity: the
   six-second kiss, the stress-reducing conversation at the end of the day, the
   ritual of reunion. Long distance, deployed, immigration-separated, work-split,
   and living-apart-together couples all fail these silently and read the failure
   as "we are behind".
2. **Assumed permanence as leverage.** Marriage advice often leans on "you are
   in this forever, so invest". Dating and unmarried cohabiting couples are
   actively deciding *whether* to be permanent. Advice framed as "make it work"
   pressures them past a real decision point.
3. **Assumed dyad.** Exercises assume the couple is the whole system. For
   stepfamilies, co-parents, caregiving couples, and CNM couples, the system has
   three or more people with standing, and dyad-only tools produce plans that
   collapse on contact with reality.
4. **Symmetry assumption.** Most tools assume both partners carry equal load and
   equal exposure. That is false where one partner is chronically ill, one is
   the immigrant, one is out and one is not, one is deployed, one is in early
   recovery, one has an executive-function disability.
5. **Pathologizing difference as conflict.** Many communication scripts treat
   difference in style, in processing speed, in emotional expression, in cultural
   norm, as a problem to resolve. For neurodivergent and intercultural couples,
   the difference is not the problem. The demand that one partner assimilate is.

## 3. The types

Format for each: **Who**, **Defining stressors**, **What generic advice gets
wrong**, **Highest-value intervention**, and where relevant a **Safety layer**.

---

### 3.1 Dating seriously, not living together

**Who.** Committed, exclusive or explicitly defined, separate homes, no legal
tie. Often the largest untapped segment for a repair app because nobody markets
repair to them.

**Defining stressors.** Ambiguity about trajectory. Unspoken timelines that do
not match. Repair is under-practiced because it is easy to end the night by
going home, so ruptures get postponed rather than closed. Each partner has an
intact independent social world that can absorb complaint and harden positions.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It assumes the goal is preservation. For
this couple the honest goal is *clarity*: are we building the same thing. Advice
that says "commit harder" skips the question. It also assumes shared finances,
shared logistics, and daily friction that this couple does not yet have, so the
classic conflict inventories come back looking suspiciously clean.

**Highest-value intervention.** A structured **trajectory conversation**: each
partner separately writes what they believe is agreed, what they hope for, and
what they have never said out loud, then reveals simultaneously. Simultaneous
reveal matters, because sequential disclosure lets the second person anchor.
Mend already has the answer-to-reveal mechanic for the daily question. Reuse it
here.

---

### 3.2 Long distance

**Who.** Committed partners in different cities or countries by circumstance,
with an expected or hoped-for close.

**Defining stressors.** Communication becomes scheduled rather than ambient, so
every call carries pressure to be good, and ordinary bad moods feel like
warnings. Idealization: partners fill gaps with a flattering imagined version of
each other, and reunion punctures it. Asymmetric sacrifice about who eventually
moves. Jealousy about an unseen daily life.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It treats distance as a deficit to be
compensated for with more contact. More contact is not the lever; **predictable,
low-stakes contact** is. Also, the standard advice ignores that the highest-risk
moment for a long distance couple is often *reunion*, not separation. Research
on reunited long distance dating couples has reported that a substantial share
break up shortly after they close the distance, once the idealized picture meets
daily life. Prevalence figures for long distance relationships in the United
States circulate widely (commonly "14 to 15 million") but trace back to
secondary and commercial sources rather than a government or peer-reviewed
count, so Mend should not publish a number.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **reunion plan built before reunion**: agree in
advance on how much time apart each person needs after moving in, who owns which
chores, and one weekly ritual that survives the move. Frame it as protecting the
thing you worked for, not as pessimism.

*See:* Stafford's overview of long distance relationships, which notes that many
studies find equal or higher satisfaction, commitment, and trust in long distance
dating relationships compared with geographically close ones.
https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/9781118540190.wbeic075

---

### 3.3 Newly cohabiting

**Who.** Moved in within roughly the last 18 months. Cohabitation is now a
majority experience: Pew, analyzing the National Survey of Family Growth,
reported that among adults ages 18 to 44, 59% have lived with an unmarried
partner at some point, a larger share than have ever been married (50%).
https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2019/11/06/key-findings-on-marriage-and-cohabitation-in-the-u-s/

**Defining stressors.** Invisible inherited defaults about cleanliness, money,
noise, guests, and alone time, none of which were ever negotiated because
neither partner knew they were choices. Loss of solitude. Merging money without
merging legal protection. Fights that look like they are about dishes.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It skips straight to communication skills for
a couple whose actual problem is **undeclared operating rules**. It also assumes
legal and financial safety nets that unmarried cohabitants do not have, which
matters enormously if things go badly.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **household operating agreement**: a short,
written, revisable list covering money, chores, guests, alone time, and how to
call a timeout. Written beats verbal here because the whole problem is that
nothing was ever made explicit.

---

### 3.4 Engaged

**Who.** Committed to marry, often deep in wedding logistics.

**Defining stressors.** The wedding eats the relationship. Family-of-origin
pressure arrives with money attached. Merged expectations about in-laws,
religion, name changes, and future children surface for the first time under a
deadline. Cold feet gets moralized instead of examined.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Premarital content is often celebratory and
avoids the questions most likely to matter, or it is checklist-driven in a way
that lets couples answer performatively. And it treats doubt as disloyalty.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **hard-questions session with permission to not
be sure**: children, money, faith, sex, in-laws, career geography, and what each
person would need if they ever became seriously ill. The explicit frame is that
"I do not know yet" is an acceptable answer and a useful one. Structured
premarital education programs such as PREP are the established model here.

---

### 3.5 Newlywed (first 2 to 3 years)

**Who.** Recently married or recently formalized, including couples who lived
together first.

**Defining stressors.** The gap between the wedding narrative and ordinary
Tuesday. Identity shift and low-grade grief for the single self, which almost
nobody admits. First real conflict about in-laws and holidays with new legal
standing. For couples who already cohabited, the anticlimax of "nothing
changed", which can read as disappointment in each other.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It frames the early years as a honeymoon, so
any difficulty is read as evidence of a mistake. It also assumes the wedding was
the start, which is false for the many couples who lived together for years.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Normalize the dip, then build one ritual.**
A single sustained weekly ritual of connection (the state-of-the-union style
check-in already in Mend) does more here than any insight, because the risk is
drift, not damage.

---

### 3.6 Long-together (10 years and up)

**Who.** Long-tenured couples, married or not, often with the original problems
still present in fossilized form.

**Defining stressors.** Accumulated unrepaired ruptures. Fixed roles that no
longer fit either person. Perpetual problems that have gone from live conflict
to silent avoidance. Reduced novelty and reduced sexual initiation. In later
life, divorce among adults 50 and older is a real and studied pattern: BGSU's
National Center for Family and Marriage Research reports that whereas 8% of
people divorcing in 1990 were aged 50 or older, that share is now near 40%.
https://www.bgsu.edu/ncfmr/resources/data/resources-by-topic/gray-divorce.html

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It aims at resolution. Most of what is left
after ten years is not resolvable, it is manageable. Advice that promises to
"fix" perpetual problems sets these couples up to fail and to conclude the
relationship is broken.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Dialogue about a gridlocked problem without
solving it**, in the Gottman sense: surface the dream or the history inside each
person's position, and aim for understanding plus a temporary compromise rather
than a solution.

---

### 3.7 Remarried, repartnered, and blended households

**Who.** At least one partner brings children or a prior long partnership.

**Defining stressors.** The couple bond is the newest bond in the house and the
weakest, while the parent-child bonds are older and stronger. Loyalty binds for
children. Discipline authority for the stepparent. A living ex-partner with
standing. Money that flows out of the household. Papernow's work describes
stepfamily integration as taking years, and identifies the stepparent-stepchild
relationship as central. Reported divorce percentages for stepfamilies vary
widely across sources and are not reliably established, so Mend should describe
the pattern qualitatively and not publish a rate.
https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/famp.12315

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Nearly all of it. "Put the marriage first"
is the single most damaging line for stepfamilies, because it asks a child to
lose a parent's attention right after a loss. "Blend" is itself a bad metaphor.
Standard couple exercises also assume the two adults have equal standing with
every child, which is false and creates unwinnable fights.

**Highest-value intervention.** **The biological parent holds discipline; the
stepparent builds relationship first.** A concrete division: for the first
stretch, the stepparent's job is warmth and shared activity, and the parent
handles rules and consequences, with both agreeing on the rules privately. Pair
it with a realistic timeline so the couple stops treating slow progress as
failure.

---

### 3.8 Queer couples

**Who.** Same-gender couples, bi and pan partners in any configuration, trans
and nonbinary partners and their partners.

**Defining stressors.** Minority stress: external discrimination, anticipation of
rejection, concealment, and internalized stigma, all of which land on the
relationship as well as the individual. A 2024 meta-analysis in *Family
Relations* pooling 67 studies (147 effect sizes, 24,542 individuals) reports that
minority stress is negatively associated with relationship satisfaction in
same-sex couples. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/fare.13068
Additional research links internalized homonegativity to lower relationship
satisfaction, with dyadic coping as a moderator.
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11562949/
Longitudinal work on same-sex couples has found that increases in experienced
stigma predict larger gaps between the closeness a person has and the closeness
they want, which in turn predicts lower satisfaction.
https://spssi.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/josi.12571
**Differing outness** is a distinct stressor: one partner fully out, the other
partly or not at all, produces asymmetric risk, asymmetric social life, and a
recurring fight that is really a negotiation over safety. Other common strains:
lack of family support, few visible long-term models, and for many couples a
smaller and overlapping social circle that includes exes.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Three specific failures. It assumes gendered
role scripts that do not apply and that quietly insult the couple. It treats
outness as a personal-growth milestone rather than a safety calculation, so
"just be honest with your family" can be dangerous advice. And it locates the
stress inside the couple when a meaningful share of it is coming from outside,
which makes partners blame each other for something the world is doing to them.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Name the outside stress as outside, then build
a joint outness and boundaries plan.** Concretely: separate the list of problems
into "ours" and "theirs", agree explicitly on who knows what, in which settings,
and who handles which family, and agree that the less-out partner sets the floor
without being punished for it. This is Gottman's stress-reducing conversation
applied to minority stress, and it turns a chronic fight into a joint plan
against a shared external pressure.

**Safety layer.** Any shared or synced content must be individually
suppressible. A partner who is not out to family may share a device or an
account. Never generate a shared summary, notification preview, or exportable
document that discloses identity by default.

---

### 3.9 Interracial and intercultural couples

**Who.** Partners of different races or ethnicities, or raised in materially
different cultures, including couples of the same race raised in different
countries or class contexts. Pew reported that 17% of United States newlyweds in
2015 married someone of a different race or ethnicity, up from 3% in 1967.
https://www.pewresearch.org/social-trends/2017/05/18/intermarriage-in-the-u-s-50-years-after-loving-v-virginia/

**Defining stressors.** External: stares, comments, family disapproval,
differential treatment in public and by institutions. Internal: differing norms
about family involvement, obligation to parents, money sharing with relatives,
directness in conflict, expression of anger, holidays, food, language at home,
and how children will be raised and named. A recurring pattern is that one
partner is expected to do all of the adapting, usually the one whose culture is
less dominant locally.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It assumes one shared conflict style is
correct and that the "healthy" style is the therapist's own, which is very often
a direct, verbal, individualist, emotionally expressive North American norm.
Advice to "set boundaries with your family" can be read as an instruction to
abandon a core obligation. And it under-weights the sheer cost of absorbing
external hostility as a couple.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **culture-of-origin mapping exercise**: each
partner writes how their family handled money, conflict, elders, privacy, and
celebration, then the couple deliberately chooses which practices to keep, which
to drop, and which to invent new. This reframes difference as design work and
kills the assimilation default.

---

### 3.10 Interfaith couples

**Who.** Partners of different religions, or one religious and one not. Pew's
2023-24 Religious Landscape Study reports 74% of married United States adults
have a spouse of the same religion and 26% do not.
https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/02/26/religious-intermarriage/

**Defining stressors.** Children: baptism, naming, circumcision, schooling,
holidays, and what the child is told is true. Extended family pressure, often
strongest at weddings, births, and funerals. Differing meanings for the same
practice. Drift, where one partner's faith deepens or lapses years in, changing
a deal that was settled at the start. Grief and end-of-life practices, which
almost never get discussed until they are urgent.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It either pushes conversion to one side or
treats religion as a preference like a food dislike. Both are wrong. It also
treats the interfaith question as settled once, when in practice it reopens at
every life transition.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **standing renegotiation ritual**: an explicit
agreement, in writing, about practice and children, plus a scheduled revisit at
named life events. The commitment is not to a permanent answer, it is to never
letting the answer change silently.

---

### 3.11 Large age gap

**Who.** Partners separated by roughly a decade or more.

**Defining stressors.** Different life phases running at once: one building a
career while the other winds down, one with young-adult friendships and one with
aging parents. Asymmetric fertility windows. Asymmetric health horizons and the
near-certainty that one will care for the other. Financial and power imbalance,
sometimes real, sometimes only perceived by outsiders. Social judgment and
family suspicion, which pushes the couple to defend rather than examine.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It treats the age gap itself as the problem
to be defended or diagnosed. The gap is rarely the live issue. The live issues
are the *phase mismatch* and the *unspoken future*. Advice also tends to assume
peer social networks and shared cultural references that these couples do not
have.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **two-timeline exercise**: each partner draws
the next 5, 15, and 30 years including health, work, parents, and money, then
they overlay the timelines and mark where the plans collide. It converts a
defensive identity topic into concrete planning.

---

### 3.12 Military, deployment, and first responders

**Who.** Active duty, Guard and Reserve, and by extension shift-working first
responders with similar rhythms.

**Defining stressors.** Cycles: pre-deployment withdrawal, separation, reunion,
reintegration. Frequent relocation that repeatedly destroys the non-serving
partner's career and social network. Role rebalancing on return, where the
at-home partner ran everything and is now asked to hand some of it back. Secrecy
and things that cannot be described. Trauma, sleep disruption, and hypervigilance
that follow the service member home. Note: numerous websites publish precise
military divorce rates and deployment risk percentages with no traceable source.
The Department of Defense Demographics Profile of the Military Community is the
appropriate primary source, and Mend should cite that directly or say nothing.
https://download.militaryonesource.mil/12038/MOS/Reports/2023-demographics-report.pdf

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It assumes continuous presence, so it has no
model for the pre-deployment pull-away, which partners routinely misread as
rejection or an affair when it is anticipatory grief. It also assumes a stable
home base and portable careers. And it treats reunion as the happy ending when
reintegration is the hardest phase.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **cycle-aware playbook**: name the four phases,
predict the pull-away out loud before it happens, and agree in advance on a
reentry protocol, including how many days before big decisions get made, who
runs which domain during and after, and what the at-home partner keeps.

**Safety layer.** Elevated relevance for trauma, sleep, alcohol, and firearms
access. Crisis resources for this segment should include the Veterans Crisis Line
alongside the general lines already in the app.

---

### 3.13 Separated by immigration or by work

**Who.** Couples apart because of visa processing, status, deportation risk, or
a job in another city or country. Overlaps long distance but is materially
different because the separation is **not chosen and often has no end date**.

**Defining stressors.** Indefinite waiting with no controllable variable. Legal
process that requires the couple to document and perform their intimacy for a
government reviewer, which is corrosive to spontaneity. Costs. Fear of denial or
removal. For work-split couples, guilt on the traveling side and resentment plus
sole-parenting exhaustion on the staying side. Research on spousal reunification
policy documents prolonged separation, emotional stress, and financial hardship
for mixed-status transnational families.
https://doi.org/10.3390/socsci14070442

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It offers coping tips that assume the
separation ends on a known date, and it frames togetherness as a decision the
couple can make. Both are false here. Advice to "prioritize the relationship
over work" is empty when the alternative is losing status or income.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Split the controllables from the
uncontrollables, out loud, together.** A two-column exercise: what we can decide
this month versus what only the system decides, plus one shared ritual that
requires no plane ticket and survives an indefinite timeline. The goal is to
stop the couple from spending their limited contact time relitigating an
unmovable object.

**Safety layer.** Immigration status is a recognized vector of coercive control
(threats to withdraw a petition or to report a partner). The DV gate copy should
include this without singling out immigrant partners as suspects.

### 3.14 Neurodivergent pairings: ADHD

**Who.** One or both partners with ADHD, diagnosed or not.

**Defining stressors.** The parent-child dynamic, where the non-ADHD partner
becomes manager and the ADHD partner becomes managed, producing chronic
resentment on one side and chronic shame on the other. Uneven invisible labor
and reminders that read as nagging. Time blindness and broken plans that are
read as not caring. Emotional intensity and rejection sensitivity turning small
critiques into large ruptures. Hyperfocus early in the relationship that fades,
which the partner experiences as bait and switch.
https://www.adhdmarriage.com/content/understanding-parent-child-dynamics-couples-adhd

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Standard advice says "try harder,
communicate more, be more consistent", which is asking an executive-function
disability to be resolved by intention. It also frames forgetting as a character
flaw about love, which is the single most damaging misreading in these
relationships. And it puts the burden of change entirely on the ADHD partner
while leaving the manager role unexamined.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Externalize the system, not the person.** Move
responsibilities into shared external structures (shared lists, alarms,
automatic transfers, a whiteboard, single-owner domains rather than shared
domains) and explicitly retire the manager role. Pair it with a rule that
symptom behavior gets named as symptom behavior, not as evidence of feeling.

---

### 3.15 Neurodivergent pairings: autism

**Who.** One or both partners autistic. Autistic-allistic pairings and
autistic-autistic pairings behave differently and should be distinguished.

**Defining stressors.** Mismatch in communication style, sensory needs, need for
routine, and social load. Meltdown and shutdown misread as withdrawal or
punishment. Direct speech read as coldness, and indirect speech missed entirely.
Masking exhaustion, where the autistic partner performs all day and has nothing
left at home. The double empathy problem, Milton's framing, holds that the
breakdown is bidirectional: allistic people are also poor at reading autistic
people, so the difficulty belongs to the pair rather than to one person.
https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8354525/
A 2025 review of autism in romantic relationships across 2013 to 2024 catalogues
both challenges and strengths, and reports higher rates of communication
difficulty specifically in mixed-neurotype pairings.
https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jftr.70001

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It prescribes eye contact, spontaneity,
extended verbal processing, and open-ended emotional questions, all of which are
costly or unhelpful for many autistic people, then reads the resistance as
avoidance. It treats one communication style as the healthy one. And it ignores
sensory reality entirely, so "have a date night at a restaurant" is a plan for a
bad night.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Build an explicit user manual for each
partner**, exchanged: how I show love, how I show overwhelm, what I need after
conflict and how long, what sensory input I cannot tolerate, which questions I
answer better in writing. Written and asynchronous by design, because that is
the accommodation. Frame the goal as translation between two valid styles,
never as one partner becoming more normal.

### 3.16 Chronic illness or disability

**Who.** One or both partners living with a chronic condition, a disability, or
a fluctuating illness.

**Defining stressors.** Role collapse from partner into patient and caregiver,
which erodes peer status and often sex. Unpredictability, where plans are
provisional and the well partner learns not to hope. Grief for a former shared
life, which both feel and neither wants to say first. Financial strain and
insurance dependence. Guilt on the ill side and unspeakable resentment on the
well side. Medical appointments consuming the couple's shared time.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It assumes symmetric capacity. Turn-taking
tools, equal-chore agreements, and "meet in the middle" advice all break here.
It also has no vocabulary for legitimate resentment, so the well partner has
nowhere to put it and either suppresses it or leaks it. And it treats caregiving
and partnership as the same relationship when they are two relationships running
on one pair of people.

**A caution on the evidence.** A widely cited 2015 study reporting that a wife's
illness onset raised divorce risk was **retracted and corrected** after a coding
error; the corrected divorce-risk estimate was far lower than the published one.
Mend must not repeat the original figures.
Retraction and authors' explanation:
https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0022146515595817
Coverage: https://retractionwatch.com/2015/07/21/to-our-horror-widely-reported-study-suggesting-divorce-is-more-likely-when-wives-fall-ill-gets-axed/

**Highest-value intervention.** **Separate the caregiving relationship from the
romantic one, deliberately and on the calendar.** Name specific hours or a
specific ritual that is partner-only, where care tasks and medical talk are off
limits, and if possible move some care load to a third party or a paid or
community resource. Add a sanctioned channel for the well partner's resentment
and the ill partner's grief that does not require the other to absorb it in the
moment.

### 3.17 Couples in recovery from addiction

**Who.** One or both partners in recovery, at any stage, from substances or
behavioral addiction.

**Defining stressors.** Trust debt from the using period, which does not clear on
the schedule the recovering partner expects. Role rigidity, where the sober
partner stays permanently in the monitor role. Early recovery reordering
priorities so that meetings and program come before the relationship, which the
partner can experience as another abandonment. Identity change: the person who
gets sober is not the person the partner married. Relapse fear coloring every
ordinary bad mood. The partner's own untreated damage, which nobody is treating.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It treats trust as restorable by apology and
by time alone. It also treats the relationship as the primary unit when in early
recovery the recovery itself has to come first, and advice that says "put your
marriage first" can be actively dangerous. Conversely, some recovery culture
sidelines the partner entirely, which is also wrong: behavioral couples therapy
for alcohol and drug use disorders has meta-analytic support showing better
substance use outcomes and better relationship functioning than individual
treatment alone.
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18374464/
Review of the O'Farrell and Fals-Stewart program of research:
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21083548/
(Note: the review itself flags that data integrity questions were raised about
one of the researchers, so present BCT as supported, not as settled.)

**Highest-value intervention.** A **written recovery contract plus a daily
check-in**, which is the core mechanic of behavioral couples therapy: the
recovering partner states their intent for the day, the other acknowledges it,
and both agree not to discuss past using or fear of future using outside a
scheduled time. It gives trust a daily observable, which is what trust actually
rebuilds on.

**Safety layer.** Relapse and overdose resources, plus a clear line that Mend
does not treat substance use disorder and cannot replace treatment.

### 3.18 Co-parenting after separation who want to rebuild

**Who.** Separated or divorced, still parenting together, exploring
reconciliation. Distinct from co-parents who only need to cooperate.

**Defining stressors.** The children are watching, so every experiment is
high-stakes and semi-public. New partners, current or recent. Legal and
financial entanglement that gives every conflict a courtroom shadow. Family and
friends who took sides and who lose credibility if the couple reconciles.
Ambiguity that harms the children most: they need to know what is happening and
the parents do not know yet.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Co-parenting content assumes the goal is
functional distance and teaches business-like disengagement, which is the exact
opposite of what a rebuilding couple needs. Meanwhile, couples content assumes an
intact household. Neither addresses the reality of trying to fall back in love
while running a custody schedule.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **private, time-boxed trial with an explicit
children protocol**: agree on a defined window, agree that nothing is announced
to the children during it, and agree in advance what a "no" would look like and
how it would be handled. Protecting the children from a second loss is the
non-negotiable, and naming it first makes the trial possible.

---

### 3.19 Broke up and are reconciling

**Who.** Previously ended, now back together or attempting it. Includes
on-and-off cycling ("relationship churn"), which is a documented pattern
associated with worse relationship quality.
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3924753/

**Defining stressors.** Whatever ended it is usually still there. Asymmetric
readiness, where one person wanted the reunion much more. What happened during
the gap, including other partners. Fear of a second ending that makes both
partners walk on eggshells and avoid the original issue. Friends and family who
are openly skeptical.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It says either "never go back" or "love
conquers all". Neither is a method. Standard advice also encourages a fresh
start with a clean slate, which sounds generous and functions as a ban on the
one conversation that has to happen.

**Estimates.** Reconciliation rates after marital separation vary enormously
across studies and samples, with published figures spanning roughly 12% to 44%
depending on definition and cohort. A recent Journal of Marriage and Family
paper on later-life separation, reconciliation, and repartnering summarizes this
spread. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jomf.13024
Mend should not publish a single reconciliation rate.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **structured post-mortem before rebuilding**:
each partner writes what they believe caused the ending, what they own, and what
specifically must be different, then compare. If the two accounts do not overlap
at all, that is the finding, and the app should say so plainly rather than
cheerlead.

---

### 3.20 Consensually non-monogamous couples

**Who.** Open, polyamorous, swinging, relationship-anarchist, and other
explicitly negotiated non-exclusive structures. Prevalence is nontrivial:
Haupert and colleagues, using two nationally representative samples of single
United States adults, found that roughly one in five reported having engaged in
consensual non-monogamy at some point.
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27096488/
The Kinsey Institute summarizes the polyamory and CNM literature here:
https://news.iu.edu/kinseyinstitute/live/news/44946-polyamory-and-consensual-non-monogamy-in-the-us

**Defining stressors.** Agreement drift, where the rules made at the start no
longer fit and nobody has reopened them. Jealousy and its inverse, which need
processing capacity that the couple may not have. Time and energy scarcity.
Hierarchy conflicts between a primary partnership and other relationships.
Stigma and concealment from family, employers, and courts, which mirrors the
minority stress pattern. Breaches of an agreement, which function as infidelity
inside this structure and are often more painful because the couple had
explicitly built a system to prevent them.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Nearly all mainstream couples content
assumes monogamy as the definition of commitment, so it reads any outside
relationship as betrayal and any negotiation as a warning sign. That framing is
both wrong and alienating for this group. It also lacks any language for
agreement breach as distinct from the structure itself.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **scheduled agreement review**: a recurring
session where the current agreements are read aloud, each partner says what is
working and what needs to change, and changes are recorded with a date. Most CNM
distress in practice comes from an unrevised agreement rather than from
non-monogamy.

**Product note.** Mend should not moralize either direction. Content should
distinguish clearly between "we agreed to this" and "this happened outside our
agreement", and the healing-after-betrayal track should be reachable from both
monogamous and CNM contexts. The DV gate applies unchanged: coerced consent to
an open relationship is coercion.

---

### 3.21 No children by choice

**Who.** Couples who have decided not to have children, or one partner who has
and one who has not yet. Pew reports the share of United States adults under 50
without children who say they are unlikely to ever have children rose from 37%
in 2018 to 47% in 2023, with 57% of the younger group naming "just don't want
to" as a major reason.
https://www.pewresearch.org/social-trends/2024/07/25/the-experiences-of-u-s-adults-who-dont-have-children/

**Defining stressors.** Persistent external pressure and the assumption that the
decision is temporary. Social drift as friends have children and disappear.
Absence of the default structure that children impose on a couple's time,
meaning connection has to be deliberately constructed. Asymmetric certainty,
where one partner is sure and the other is quietly not. Later-life questions
about care, legacy, and who will be there.

**What generic advice gets wrong.** Almost all couples content uses children as
the organizing life stage, the source of conflict examples, and the assumed
shared project. Childfree couples read past a constant stream of content that is
not for them. Advice also frames the childfree decision as an open question to
keep revisiting, which is disrespectful when it is settled.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **shared-project exercise**: choose one
concrete thing the two of you are building together over the next several years,
and give it a name, a calendar, and a ritual. It replaces the structure that
children otherwise impose. Second priority is a scripted, agreed response to
outside pressure so neither partner is left improvising alone at family
gatherings.

---

### 3.22 Empty nesters

**Who.** Last child has left home or is leaving.

**Defining stressors.** Discovering that the shared project was the children, and
that little else was built. Renegotiating an entire daily structure at once.
Grief that is socially expected to be relief. Often simultaneous with menopause
or andropause, retirement, and aging parents. Sexual reconnection after years of
low privacy, which requires actual effort and can be embarrassing to initiate.
This is also the life stage where later-life divorce concentrates.
https://www.bgsu.edu/ncfmr/resources/data/resources-by-topic/gray-divorce.html

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It says "rediscover each other" without
acknowledging that the people who married are gone and the task is closer to
meeting someone new. It also assumes the nest is empty, when boomerang adult
children and simultaneous elder care are extremely common, so the promised
freedom never arrives.

**Highest-value intervention.** A **love-maps update run as if for a stranger**:
current dreams, current fears, current daily texture, deliberately not the
answers from twenty years ago. Followed by one new shared activity chosen
together and started within two weeks, because insight without a new structure
does not survive contact with an empty evening.

---

### 3.23 Caregiving couples

**Who.** One or both partners providing unpaid care to a parent, an adult child,
or each other. The AARP and National Alliance for Caregiving 2020 report
estimated 53.0 million adults in the United States were unpaid caregivers, up
from 43.5 million in 2015, with 24% caring for more than one person.
https://www.caregiving.org/research/caregiving-in-the-us/caregiving-in-the-us-2020/
Full report PDF:
https://www.aarp.org/content/dam/aarp/ppi/2020/05/full-report-caregiving-in-the-united-states.doi.10.26419-2Fppi.00103.001.pdf

**Defining stressors.** Total time collapse: no unclaimed hours exist. Sandwich
load with children and parents at once. Conflict over how much care to give and
to whose parent, which is really a conflict about loyalty. Financial drain.
Physical exhaustion and sleep loss, which erode conflict tolerance more than any
communication skill can compensate for. Anticipatory grief. Guilt that makes it
impossible to say "I need a break".

**What generic advice gets wrong.** It prescribes time-intensive interventions
(weekly date night, hour-long conversations, weekend getaways) to the one
population with no time. Advice to "make yourselves a priority" reads as an
accusation. It also assumes the couple's stressor can be reduced by talking
about it, when the stressor is a fixed number of hours of required labor.

**Highest-value intervention.** **Micro-connection plus one logistics
conversation.** Two-minute rituals that fit inside a caregiving day (a specific
morning question, a fixed touch point, a shared note), plus a single structured
conversation whose only job is to redistribute or outsource one recurring task,
not to process feelings. Then, and only then, feelings. Reduce the load before
asking for emotional work.

## 4. Cross-cutting product recommendations

1. **Ship the tag model, not 22 journeys.** One journey, tag-aware context
   layers. Otherwise content debt compounds and quality drops.
2. **Rewrite the base library to be structurally neutral first.** Remove
   assumptions of shared address, legal marriage, children, gender roles, and
   monogamy from the *default* copy, then add specificity as layers. This is
   cheaper and better than writing variants.
3. **Three highest-leverage new mechanics**, each of which serves many types at
   once:
   - **Simultaneous reveal** (already built for the daily question): serves
     dating, reconciling, engaged, interfaith, age gap, post-mortem work.
   - **Written asynchronous exchange**: serves autism, ADHD, long distance,
     deployment, immigration separation, and anyone who processes badly out loud.
   - **Written, dated, revisable agreements**: serves cohabiting, CNM, interfaith,
     stepfamilies, recovery, and caregiving. One component, six audiences.
4. **Micro-format tier.** Caregiving, military, chronic illness, and new-parent
   couples need two-minute exercises. Anything that requires 45 uninterrupted
   minutes excludes them entirely.
5. **Per-item privacy on shared spaces.** Required by outness asymmetry, by
   immigration status, by recovery disclosure, and by anyone who shares a device.
6. **Sensitive-tag handling.** Queer, not-out, immigration, recovery, disability,
   and CNM tags should be treated as sensitive: local-first, never in a default
   shared summary, never in a push notification preview.
7. **Never publish an unsourced statistic in-app.** Several categories above have
   only content-farm numbers available. The right move is qualitative language
   ("many couples find") rather than a fabricated precision.

## 5. Safety posture, unchanged and extended

Everything below is additive to the existing posture (deal contract, domestic
violence gate, per-track red flags, crisis lines, "educational tool, not
therapy"). Nothing here weakens or replaces it, and none of it goes behind a
paywall.

- The DV gate applies to every relationship type without exception.
- Coercive control has type-specific forms that the gate copy should name in
  plain language: threatening a partner's immigration status or visa petition;
  outing or threatening to out a partner; withholding medication, mobility
  equipment, or care; controlling access to recovery meetings or medication;
  coercing consent to an open relationship; weaponizing custody.
- Crisis resources should include, alongside the general lines already present,
  a veterans line for the military segment and a substance use helpline for the
  recovery segment.
- Reconciliation content must never pressure. For the reconciling, co-parenting,
  and separated tracks, "the honest answer may be no" has to appear in the
  content itself, not only in a disclaimer.
- Mend does not treat substance use disorder, mental illness, trauma, or
  disability. Say so in the tracks where a user is most likely to hope
  otherwise.
