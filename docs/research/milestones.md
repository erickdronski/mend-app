# What real progress looks like in relationship repair

Research brief for Mend's achievement system.
Written 2026-07-19. All sources were accessed on that date.

This document answers three questions, then converts the answers into 40 candidate
achievements.

1. What early signs actually indicate a couple is improving?
2. What is a realistic timeline for change?
3. What do therapists look for session to session?

**Scope note.** Mend serves all committed romantic relationships. Most of the
research below was conducted on married, mostly heterosexual, mostly US samples.
That is a real limitation of the evidence base, not a statement about who this
app is for. Where a study's sample is narrow, it is said plainly.

**Framing note.** Nothing in here predicts whether a specific relationship will
last. The app must never imply that it can. See "Numbers we will not state" at
the end.

---

## Part 1: Early markers that a couple is actually improving

### 1.1 Repair attempts that land

The most consistently cited early marker is not the absence of conflict, it is
what happens inside conflict. Gottman's work centers on "repair attempts": any
move that de-escalates in the middle of a fight, such as humor, a softening of
tone, taking responsibility for a piece of it, or an explicit request to slow
down.

The important detail for an app: what matters is whether the repair is
**received**, not how many are attempted. A repair that gets swatted away is not
progress. A repair that gets accepted, even clumsily, is.

- Gottman Institute, "The 6 Things That Predict Divorce": https://www.gottman.com/blog/the-6-things-that-predict-divorce/
- Overview of repair as a mid-conflict skill: https://www.howcommunicationworks.com/blog/2021/1/4/relationship-communication-john-gottmans-repair-attempts

### 1.2 Softened startup

How a difficult conversation opens strongly shapes how it goes. A "soft startup"
states the speaker's own feeling and a specific need, without a character verdict
about the partner. This is presented in the Gottman literature as the direct
counter to criticism.

For measurement purposes, softened startup is one of the few markers a person can
practice and self-observe alone, before the partner does anything differently.

- https://empathi.com/blog/what-is-the-soft-startup/

### 1.3 Reduced negative reciprocity

Negative reciprocity is the tit for tat loop: complaint meets counter complaint,
escalating. It is one of the better documented predictors of relationship
deterioration, and reducing it is one of the earliest observable changes when
therapy is working.

- Literature review, "The negative reciprocity process in marital relationships",
  Clinical Psychology Review: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1359178915000701

### 1.4 Naming the cycle, and lowered reactivity

In Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT), Stage 1 is de-escalation. The couple comes
to see the recurring pattern itself as the problem, rather than each other. The
first observable marker of change is usually a drop in emotional reactivity once
the cycle has been named out loud.

Practically: partners start saying "we're in it again" instead of "you always."

- EFT stages overview: https://bettercouplestherapy.com/steps-and-stages/
- EFT Stage 1 de-escalation: https://www.psychotherapy.net/video/EFT-stage1-deescalation

### 1.5 Bids for connection turned toward

Gottman's Love Lab followed 130 newlywed couples and reported that, at six year
follow up, couples still together had turned toward each other's small bids for
attention about 86% of the time, while couples who divorced averaged about 33%.

Treat this as directional, not as a scoring rule. It is reported through the
Gottman Institute's own materials rather than an independent replication, and the
broader prediction literature it belongs to has been criticized (see 1.8).

- https://www.gottman.com/blog/turn-toward-instead-of-away/
- https://www.cnn.com/2024/06/24/health/bids-for-connection-better-relationship-wellness

### 1.6 The positive to negative ratio during conflict

Gottman and Levenson's early observational work produced the widely repeated 5:1
ratio: stable couples showed roughly five positive interactions for every
negative one **during conflict discussions**. The original sample was small
(reported as 73 couples).

Useful as a direction of attention. Useless and slightly toxic as a live
scoreboard. Mend should never display a running ratio.

- Secondary summary with methodology: https://therapydave.com/gottman/5-to-1-ratio-relationship-guide/
- https://www.gottman.com/blog/the-magic-ratio-the-key-to-relationship-satisfaction/

### 1.7 Responsiveness, gratitude, and capitalization

Three separate research lines converge on the same everyday behavior: noticing
your partner and showing them you noticed.

- **Perceived partner responsiveness** (feeling understood, validated, cared for)
  is treated in relationship science as a core driver of satisfaction, intimacy,
  and stability. Reis, Clark and Holmes: https://www.sas.rochester.edu/psy/people/faculty/reis_harry/assets/pdf/ReisClarkHolmes_2004.pdf
- **Gratitude** that names a specific act ("you handled the call so I could
  sleep") outperforms generic thanks. Algoe's find, remind, bind theory:
  https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1751-9004.2012.00439.x
  and "Putting the You in Thank You": https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4988174/
- **Capitalization**, how you respond to your partner's *good* news, predicts
  relationship well being independently of how you handle conflict. Gable et al.,
  "What do you do when things go right?": https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15301629/

This matters for Mend because all three are practiceable by one partner alone and
require no agreement from the other.

### 1.8 A necessary caveat on prediction

Several famous divorce prediction accuracy figures did not survive scrutiny.
Heyman and Slep showed that prediction accuracy drops sharply once equations are
cross validated and base rates are accounted for. Kim, Capaldi and Crosby later
failed to replicate the affective process models in an independent sample.

- Heyman and Slep, "The Hazards of Predicting Divorce Without Crossvalidation":
  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1622921/
- Kim, Capaldi and Crosby, generalizability study:
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1741-3737.2006.00343.x

**Product consequence.** Mend celebrates *behaviors that appear in couples who do
well*. It never forecasts an outcome for the user. The distinction is the whole
ballgame for both honesty and safety.

## Part 2: Realistic timelines

### 2.1 Course length

Structured couples work is typically measured in months, not weeks. EFT is
usually described as an 8 to 20 session model. The largest behavioral couple
therapy trial ran roughly 8 months of treatment.

- EFT session range: https://www.theraplatform.com/blog/1022/emotionally-focused-couples-therapy
- Christensen et al. 2010, 5 year follow up of 134 chronically and seriously
  distressed couples: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20350033/

### 2.2 Change shows up early, or often not at all

This is the single most useful timeline finding for an app.

Pepping, Halford and Doss (2015) monitored 136 couples weekly in an effectiveness
trial. An algorithm could accurately predict eventual outcome for about 70% of
couples by **session 4**, with little improvement in prediction after that.
Gains, when they come, tend to concentrate early.

- Pepping, Halford and Doss, Behaviour Research and Therapy 65, 60 to 66:
  https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0005796714002149
- Halford 2016, efficacy versus effectiveness gap:
  https://www.aetsbtraining.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Halford-2016efficacy-effectiveness-gap.pdf

**Product consequence.** Mend's first four weeks are the highest leverage weeks
in the whole product. That is where the achievement density should be highest,
and where a "nothing is shifting yet" off ramp toward professional help should be
built in, gently and without shame.

### 2.3 What outcomes look like at the end

- EFT: Wiebe and Johnson's review reports roughly 70 to 75% of treated couples
  moving from distress to recovery across trials, with gains generally holding at
  two year follow up: https://drrebeccajorgensen.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/Review-of-EFT-Research_Wiebe-and-Johnson..pdf
- IBCT and TBCT at five years: effect sizes of d = 1.03 and d = 0.92 on marital
  satisfaction, with 50.0% and 45.9% of couples showing clinically significant
  improvement: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20350033/

Read those two together honestly: roughly half of seriously distressed couples in
the best studied trial were clearly better five years on. That is a real,
meaningful, and *partial* result. Mend's copy should sound like that number, not
like a marketing page.

### 2.4 What a self guided program can do

Most relevant benchmark for Mend. Doss et al. ran a randomized controlled trial
of the web based OurRelationship program: 300 couples, an 8 hour online program
plus four 15 minute staff calls, versus a two month waitlist. Reported effects:
relationship satisfaction d = 0.69, relationship confidence d = 0.47, negative
relationship quality d = 0.57.

- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26999504/
- https://www.ovid.com/journals/jccpy/fulltext/10.1037/ccp0000063~a-randomized-controlled-trial-of-the-web-based

**Product consequence.** A structured self guided program with a small amount of
human contact produced a medium to large effect. Mend has no human contact, so it
should expect less, and should say so. But the shape of the intervention, short
structured activities over roughly 6 to 8 weeks, is validated.

### 2.5 How long a new habit takes

Lally et al. tracked 96 people forming daily habits over 12 weeks. Median time to
reach 95% of maximum automaticity was 66 days, with a range from 18 to 254 days,
and roughly half of participants did not reach the plateau at all.

- https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/ejsp.674

**Product consequence.** Streak achievements should have generous milestones
(7, 21, 66) and must be forgiving. A broken streak that resets to zero punishes
exactly the people who need the app most.

## Part 3: What therapists look for session to session

Synthesized from the EFT stage model, the couple therapy progress monitoring
literature, and the behavioral couple therapy trials cited above.

| Session window | What a clinician is watching for |
| --- | --- |
| 1 to 2 | Both partners show up and speak. Safety screening completed. Some hope expressed. A shared, non blaming description of the problem starts to form. |
| 3 to 4 | The couple can name the cycle. Reactivity in session drops slightly. Homework gets attempted at all. This is the go or no go window (Pepping et al.). |
| 4 to 8 | Negative reciprocity shortens: fights get shorter, not necessarily rarer. Repair attempts start being accepted. Startups soften. Partners begin saying "I felt scared" rather than only "you did." |
| 8 to 15 | Softer emotions are voiced directly to the partner rather than to the therapist. Each partner can state the other's position accurately without agreeing with it. Specific injuries get named. |
| 15 and beyond | Reaching for each other under stress instead of away. Rituals hold without prompting. The couple handles a new conflict between sessions and reports it, rather than bringing in the wreckage. |
| Throughout | Alliance with both partners, not one. Weekly self report movement on satisfaction. Honest reporting, including reporting that things got worse. |

Routine outcome monitoring with feedback to the therapist reliably improves
outcomes, and works best when off track cases are caught early.

- Progress monitoring with couple clients: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32799770/
- Toward making progress feedback an effective common factor:
  https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0005789411000839

### The safety line that does not move

Conjoint couples work is generally contraindicated where intimate partner
violence is present, and guidance is that partners should be screened
individually, not in front of each other. In practice, fewer than 4% of surveyed
couple therapists reported consistently following universal screening guidelines,
which is exactly why a consumer app must not be casual here.

- Hurless and Cottone, considerations of conjoint couples therapy in cases of
  IPV: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1066480718795708
- Screening couples for intimate partner violence:
  https://www.researchgate.net/publication/233068639_Screening_Couples_for_Intimate_Partner_Violence

**Product consequence.** Mend's existing DV gate, per track red flags, crisis
resources, and "this is an educational tool, not therapy" framing are load
bearing. No achievement may be awarded for staying in a conversation that has
become unsafe, for "not leaving," for tolerating, or for pushing through. No
safety content goes behind the paywall, ever.

## Part 4: Design rules for the achievement system

Derived from everything above.

1. **Celebrate the attempt, never the outcome.** "You practiced a soft start" is
   honest. "Your relationship is healthier" is not something the app can know.
2. **Never score the relationship.** No health percentage, no compatibility
   grade, no ratio meter. Pulse checks stay private, directional, and honest.
3. **Repair received beats repair attempted.** Where both partners are present,
   the higher achievement is the one the partner confirms landed.
4. **One person can start.** At least half the achievements must be reachable
   solo, because one willing partner is the actual starting condition for most
   users.
5. **Reporting that things got worse is progress.** Honest low pulse scores earn
   recognition, never a penalty or a broken streak.
6. **Asking for outside help is a top tier achievement.** It is never a failure
   state, never a churn event to be discouraged, never hidden.
7. **No comparison, no leaderboards, no partner scoreboards.** Nothing that lets
   one partner see they are "ahead."
8. **Detection must be honest about its limits.** The app can only observe in app
   actions and self report. Copy should say "you logged" or "you marked," never
   claim to have observed real life.
9. **Forgiving streaks.** Missing days pauses, it does not erase. "You came back"
   is itself an achievement.
10. **Graduation is a goal, not a loss.** The best outcome is a user who stops
    needing the app.

## Part 5: 40 candidate achievements

Legend: **SOLO** is reachable by one partner acting alone. **BOTH** requires two
joined partners in a Couple Space.

Detection notes assume Mend's existing surfaces: journey stages, guided sessions,
card decks, challenge weeks, the lens quiz, rituals, pulse checks, the daily
question, shared notes, healing tracks, and the safety screen.

---

### Group A. Showing up

**1. First Honest Answer** (SOLO)
- Behavior: finished onboarding, including the safety contract, and answered the
  first daily question truthfully rather than performing.
- Why it matters: entry into any structured program is the largest single filter.
  Couples who never start cannot benefit, and the first honest disclosure is the
  smallest possible unit of self disclosure, which is step one of the intimacy
  process model (Reis).
- Detection: onboarding complete plus one daily answer submitted. Honor system on
  honesty, and the copy should say so.

**2. You Came Back** (SOLO)
- Behavior: returned and did anything at all after a gap of seven or more days.
- Why it matters: dropout is the primary failure mode of self guided programs.
  Relapse and return is the normal shape of behavior change, not a failure.
- Detection: session gap greater than 7 days followed by any completed action.
  Award silently and warmly, with no guilt copy about the gap.

**3. Four Weeks In** (SOLO)
- Behavior: completed meaningful activity in four separate weeks.
- Why it matters: Pepping, Halford and Doss found eventual outcome was predictable
  for about 70% of couples by session 4. Whatever is going to move usually starts
  moving by now.
- Detection: activity in four distinct ISO weeks. Pair the award with an honest
  prompt: if nothing feels different yet, here is what that usually means, and
  here is how to find a professional.

**4. Sixty Six Days** (SOLO)
- Behavior: practiced one chosen micro move on 66 days, not necessarily
  consecutive.
- Why it matters: Lally et al. found a median of 66 days to near maximum
  automaticity, range 18 to 254. This is the honest number for "it became a
  habit."
- Detection: cumulative day count on a single selected micro move. Cumulative, not
  consecutive, on purpose.

**5. Told the Truth on the Pulse** (SOLO)
- Behavior: completed a pulse check where the score went down from last time.
- Why it matters: routine outcome monitoring only works if the reports are honest.
  Accurate negative reporting is what lets a therapist, or an app, change course.
- Detection: pulse submitted with a lower mean than the previous pulse. Copy must
  be gentle and must never read as congratulating the decline.

**6. Asked for Help** (SOLO)
- Behavior: opened the professional help resources and saved or shared one.
- Why it matters: self guided tools have a ceiling. Roughly half of seriously
  distressed couples in the best studied trial were clearly better at five years,
  and that was with a trained therapist for eight months. Escalation is the
  correct move, not a defeat.
- Detection: resource screen opened plus save or share action. This should be one
  of the most prominently celebrated achievements in the app.

### Group B. Seeing the pattern

**7. Named the Cycle** (SOLO)
- Behavior: wrote a description of your recurring loop in your own words.
- Why it matters: naming the cycle is the core task of EFT Stage 1, and reduced
  reactivity after naming is often the first observable marker of change.
- Detection: free text saved in the cycle exercise, minimum length enforced only
  as a nudge, never as a gate.

**8. It Is the Cycle, Not You** (BOTH)
- Behavior: both partners independently described the same pattern.
- Why it matters: shared, externalized problem definition is the hinge of
  de-escalation. The enemy becomes the pattern.
- Detection: both partners have saved a cycle description and both have marked
  agreement after the reveal. Use answer to reveal, same as the daily question, so
  neither anchors the other.

**9. Caught It Mid Air** (SOLO)
- Behavior: logged a moment you noticed the cycle starting while it was happening.
- Why it matters: in session, clinicians look for the shift from post hoc
  recognition to real time recognition. That shift precedes behavior change.
- Detection: an in the moment log entry tagged "happening now."

**10. Spotted My Own Move** (SOLO)
- Behavior: named your own contribution, defensiveness, stonewalling, criticism,
  or contempt, rather than your partner's.
- Why it matters: negative reciprocity is a two person process. Owning one side of
  it is the only side you can control.
- Detection: self tagged entry where the subject is the user, not the partner.

**11. The Fight Autopsy** (BOTH)
- Behavior: completed a structured review of a real past fight without restarting
  it.
- Why it matters: processing a regrettable incident after both people are calm is
  a standard clinical exercise and is safe in a way that live conflict is not.
- Detection: both partners completed the unfinished fight session. Include an exit
  ramp at every step for either person to stop.

**12. Knew What They Would Say** (BOTH)
- Behavior: in the guessing game, correctly predicted your partner's answer.
- Why it matters: accurate knowledge of a partner's inner world, love maps, is a
  foundation layer, and accurate prediction of the partner's position is something
  clinicians listen for mid course.
- Detection: game result recorded. Keep it playful, no cumulative accuracy score.

**13. Updated the Map** (SOLO)
- Behavior: recorded something you learned about your partner that you did not
  know before.
- Why it matters: distressed couples run on outdated models of each other. Model
  updating is a live sign of curiosity returning.
- Detection: entry saved to the "new to me" list.

### Group C. Changing how you fight

**14. Soft Start** (SOLO)
- Behavior: drafted a hard thing using the soft startup structure, your feeling
  plus the specific situation plus a positive need, before raising it.
- Why it matters: softened startup is presented as the direct counter to criticism
  and is associated with better conversation outcomes.
- Detection: soft startup composer completed with all three fields filled.

**15. Complaint, Not Verdict** (SOLO)
- Behavior: rewrote a character judgment into a specific complaint about a
  specific behavior.
- Why it matters: the criticism to complaint conversion is the mechanical version
  of the same skill, and it is trainable alone.
- Detection: rewrite tool used, before and after both saved.

**16. Called the Break** (SOLO)
- Behavior: started the in app break timer during a real conflict.
- Why it matters: when physiological arousal spikes, listening and problem solving
  degrade. Stopping is the skill.
- Detection: break timer started. Note in copy that the arousal recovery window is
  clinical guidance, not a precise law of the body.
- Reference: https://www.gottman.com/blog/does-flooding-play-a-role-in-your-perpetual-conflict/

**17. Actually Soothed** (SOLO)
- Behavior: completed a calming activity during the break instead of rehearsing
  your argument.
- Why it matters: the break only works if it is not used for rumination. This is
  the part people skip.
- Detection: a calming exercise completed inside an active break window.

**18. Came Back to It** (SOLO)
- Behavior: reopened the conversation within 24 hours of calling a break.
- Why it matters: a break that becomes avoidance is stonewalling with better
  branding. Return is what makes the break a repair.
- Detection: break closed with "we came back" marked within 24 hours.

**19. Shorter Than Last Time** (SOLO)
- Behavior: logged a conflict that ended sooner than your usual.
- Why it matters: when therapy works, fights typically get shorter before they get
  rarer. Duration drops before frequency does.
- Detection: self reported duration compared to the user's own prior entries.
  Compare only to the user's own history, never to other users.

**20. Said the Thing Underneath** (SOLO)
- Behavior: named the softer feeling under the anger, such as scared, lonely, or
  not important to you.
- Why it matters: the move from secondary reactive emotion to primary attachment
  emotion is the central mechanism of EFT.
- Detection: entry saved in the underneath exercise with a primary emotion
  selected.

**21. Said It To Them** (BOTH)
- Behavior: shared that softer feeling directly with your partner rather than only
  logging it privately.
- Why it matters: in session, clinicians specifically watch for the moment a
  partner turns and says it to the other person instead of about them.
- Detection: an underneath entry explicitly shared to the Couple Space and marked
  received.

### Group D. Repair

**22. Reached Out Mid Fight** (SOLO)
- Behavior: used a repair phrase during a live conflict.
- Why it matters: mid conflict de-escalation is the behavior most consistently
  associated with couples who do well.
- Detection: repair phrase sent or logged with the conflict marked as active.

**23. It Landed** (BOTH)
- Behavior: your partner marked that your repair reached them.
- Why it matters: acceptance, not frequency, is the marker. A hundred rejected
  repairs is not progress.
- Detection: partner taps "that helped" on a repair. Only the partner can award
  it. No nagging, no reminder pressure.

**24. Let It Land** (SOLO)
- Behavior: accepted your partner's repair when you did not feel like it.
- Why it matters: receiving is a skill of its own, and it is the half of the
  exchange the receiver controls.
- Detection: user marked a received repair as accepted.

**25. Both Directions** (BOTH)
- Behavior: each partner has now both offered and accepted at least one repair.
- Why it matters: one sided repair is a common stuck pattern where one person
  does all the softening. Bidirectionality is the actual milestone.
- Detection: matrix of four states filled, offered and accepted for each partner.

**26. Owned One Thing** (SOLO)
- Behavior: apologized for one specific thing with no "but" attached.
- Why it matters: taking responsibility for a piece of it is one of the repair
  categories, and the unqualified version is what distinguishes it from a
  counterattack.
- Detection: apology composer used, with a lightweight check that flags "but" and
  offers a rewrite rather than blocking.

**27. Took the Influence** (SOLO)
- Behavior: logged a decision where you changed your position because of your
  partner's view.
- Why it matters: willingness to be influenced by a partner is a repeatedly
  emphasized finding in the newlywed interaction work, and sharing power is the
  behavioral form of it.
- Detection: entry in the "you changed my mind" log.
- Reference: https://www.washington.edu/news/1998/02/20/husbands-willingness-to-be-influenced-by-wife-share-power-are-key-predictors-of-newlywed-happiness-stability-uw-study-shows/

**28. Asked Instead of Assumed** (SOLO)
- Behavior: asked a clarifying question instead of acting on what you assumed they
  meant.
- Why it matters: negative interpretation drives negative reciprocity. Checking
  the interpretation is where the loop can be cut.
- Detection: use of the check it prompt, logged.

### Group E. Turning toward

**29. Ten Small Yeses** (SOLO)
- Behavior: noticed and turned toward ten small bids for attention.
- Why it matters: bids are the everyday, low cost, high frequency unit of
  connection, and turning toward them is the behavior the Love Lab follow up
  highlighted.
- Detection: ten bid log entries. Keep the logging to one tap.

**30. Turning Toward Week** (BOTH)
- Behavior: both partners completed the turning toward challenge week.
- Why it matters: doing it as a pair changes it from private practice into a
  shared norm.
- Detection: challenge week marked complete by both.

**31. Specific Thanks, Five Times** (SOLO)
- Behavior: wrote five appreciations that each name a concrete action.
- Why it matters: gratitude that praises the specific act, rather than a generic
  compliment, is the version associated with relationship benefit.
- Detection: five appreciation notes saved, with a soft nudge when a note contains
  no verb.

**32. Good News, Well Received** (SOLO)
- Behavior: responded to your partner's good news with active interest, questions
  and attention, rather than a flat acknowledgment.
- Why it matters: how partners respond to good news predicts relationship well
  being separately from how they handle conflict. This is the most overlooked
  skill in the whole space.
- Detection: capitalization practice completed, self reported afterward.

**33. Stress Dumped, Nothing Fixed** (BOTH)
- Behavior: completed the outside stress conversation where offering solutions is
  off limits.
- Why it matters: this is a pure perceived responsiveness exercise. It builds the
  felt sense of being understood without requiring either person to concede
  anything about the relationship itself.
- Detection: both partners marked the session complete. Best possible first shared
  session because there is nothing to lose an argument about.

**34. Something New Together** (BOTH)
- Behavior: did a novel, mildly exciting activity together and logged it.
- Why it matters: Aron et al. found that even a seven minute novel and arousing
  shared task raised experienced relationship quality relative to a mundane task,
  across three experiments.
- Detection: both partners logged the same activity.
- Reference: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10707334/

**35. Held for Four Weeks** (BOTH)
- Behavior: kept the same weekly check in ritual four weeks running.
- Why it matters: clinicians watch for rituals that persist without prompting.
  Persistence without the therapist is the handover point.
- Detection: four consecutive weekly rituals logged by both. Allow one grace week
  without breaking it.

### Group F. The heavy work

**36. Opened the Hard Door** (SOLO)
- Behavior: opened a healing track (betrayal, addiction, grief, illness, and so
  on) and read its red flags in full.
- Why it matters: for many users the presenting problem is a specific wound, and
  reading the contraindications first is the responsible entry.
- Detection: track opened and red flag screen scrolled to completion. Never
  paywalled.

**37. Named the Moment** (SOLO)
- Behavior: described the specific moment you needed them and they were not there.
- Why it matters: an attachment injury is a discrete incident where the
  expectation of comfort was violated at a moment of urgent need. Specificity is
  what makes it workable rather than a permanent atmosphere.
- Detection: injury description saved.
- Reference: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/6644242_Resolving_Attachment_Injuries_in_Couples_Using_Emotionally_Focused_Therapy_Steps_Toward_Forgiveness_and_Reconciliation

**38. Stayed in the Room** (BOTH)
- Behavior: both partners completed a conversation they each marked as hard,
  without either one leaving it unfinished.
- Why it matters: the attachment injury resolution work depends on the injured
  partner voicing the need and the other partner staying present for it.
- Detection: both marked difficulty high and both marked complete. Hard
  requirement: the exit ramp and crisis resources are visible on every screen of
  this flow, and using the exit ramp must never break a streak or forfeit an
  achievement.

**39. Money, Out Loud** and **Desire, Out Loud** (BOTH)
- Behavior: completed the money history session, or the affection and desire
  session.
- Why it matters: money and sex are the two topics couples most reliably avoid,
  and avoidance itself maintains distress. Getting the conversation started at all
  is the milestone, agreement is not required.
- Detection: session marked complete by both. Award for completion, never for
  reaching consensus.

**40. Found the Dream Underneath** (BOTH)
- Behavior: completed the gridlock session and each named the deeper hope or fear
  inside a position you have fought about for years.
- Why it matters: many perpetual problems are not solvable, and the clinical goal
  shifts from solving to understanding. Reaching that reframe is a genuine late
  stage milestone.
- Detection: both partners saved a dream statement in the gridlock session.

## Part 6: Achievements we deliberately will not build

- Anything named or phrased as "your relationship is healthy now."
- A relationship score, grade, percentage, or ratio meter.
- A perfect streak that resets to zero and shames the user.
- Anything awarded for enduring, tolerating, staying, or not leaving.
- Anything that compares one partner to the other, or one couple to others.
- Anything that rewards a partner for pressuring the other to participate.
- Anything gated behind payment that touches safety, crisis resources, red flags,
  or the DV gate.
- Anything that claims the app predicted or measured a real world outcome it
  cannot observe.

---

## Numbers we will not state publicly

These circulate widely and are either unverified by us or contested in the
literature. Do not put them in app copy, marketing, or the App Store listing.

- "Gottman predicts divorce with 90 something percent accuracy" in any form,
  including the three minutes and 96% version and the 93.6% version. Prediction
  accuracy fell sharply under cross validation (Heyman and Slep) and the affective
  process models failed to replicate independently (Kim, Capaldi and Crosby).
- "81% of marriages self destruct when men refuse to accept influence." We could
  not verify this figure against the primary source and it should not be used.
- Any success rate for Mend itself. We have no outcome data.

## The one figure that is safe to state publicly

> In a randomized controlled trial of the web based OurRelationship program, 300
> couples who completed an 8 hour online program reported significantly greater
> improvement in relationship satisfaction than couples on a waitlist, with an
> effect size of d = 0.69.
>
> Source: Doss et al., Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 2016.
> https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26999504/ (accessed 2026-07-19)

If used in marketing, it must be stated as evidence that structured self guided
relationship programs can help in general, not as a claim about Mend. Mend has no
outcome data of its own, and saying otherwise would be a fabrication.
