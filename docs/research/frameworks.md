# Evidence-based frameworks for a relationship repair app

Research brief for Mend. Written 2026-07-19. Every figure below carries a source
URL and the date it was read. Where a popular claim could not be verified against
a credible source, this document says so plainly instead of repeating it.

Scope note: Mend now serves all committed romantic relationships, not only
marriages. Much of the strongest research was run on married, mostly white,
mostly different-sex, mostly middle-income samples. That limitation is called out
throughout, because it changes what the app is allowed to claim.

---

## 0. How to use this document

Three tiers govern what the app may say.

**Tier A, state as fact.** Replicated across independent labs, or established in
large randomized trials, or reported in a peer-reviewed meta-analysis. The app
may state it, with attribution.

**Tier B, state as "one way to think about it".** Clinically useful models with
partial or developer-run evidence. The app may teach the practice without
claiming it is proven to cause a result.

**Tier C, never state as fact.** Popular claims whose evidence base does not hold
up. The app may not use these as headlines, quiz results, scores, or marketing.
Section 7 is the do-not-say list.

**The default framing.** Mend is an educational tool, not therapy, and not a
diagnostic. Nothing in this document changes that. Nothing here weakens the
domestic violence gate, the crisis resources, or the per-track red flags.

## 1. The honest baseline: what couple interventions actually achieve

This is the single most important section for product decisions, because it sets
the ceiling on what an app can promise.

The best available synthesis is Bradbury and Bodenmann, "Interventions for
Couples", Annual Review of Clinical Psychology, 2020, volume 16, pages 99 to 123.
Read 2026-07-19 at
https://marriage.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/213/2021/04/Bradbury-Bodenmann-ARCP-2020.pdf

Key figures, all quoted or closely paraphrased from that review:

- Across meta-analyses of randomized trials, "the average distressed partner
  reports better relationship functioning at the end of treatment than 80% of
  otherwise identical couples receiving no treatment (mean d = 0.84)", citing
  Shadish and Baldwin 2003.
- 60 to 80 percent of distressed couples benefit from behavioral and
  emotion-focused couple therapy.
- Effects are weaker in real clinical practice than in controlled trials, and
  "dissipate following treatment for about half of all couples".
- In naturalistic settings, about half of couples fail to complete therapy, up to
  40 percent reach a non-clinical level of adjustment by their last session, and
  30 to 60 percent of those relapse to pre-treatment levels at some point after
  termination, citing Roesler 2019.
- In the largest head-to-head trial, at termination 48 percent of behavioral and
  integrative behavioral couple therapy couples had recovered, 18 percent
  improved without full recovery, 21 percent did not change, and 13 percent
  deteriorated (N = 130).
- Head-to-head comparisons of different couple therapy models "rarely reveal any
  approach as superior".
- Effects "may be explained by factors that are common across models".

**Product consequence.** The strongest, most professional version of this work
helps most but not all couples, and about half of the gains fade. An app must
never promise more than a therapist can deliver. Mend's existing graduation logic,
which tells couples honestly when the numbers do not support graduation, is
correctly calibrated to this evidence. Deterioration is a real outcome in
13 percent of treated couples, which is a strong argument for keeping the
"pause and get a professional" off-ramps prominent.

## 2. Gottman method

### 2.1 What it is

A set of observational findings from John Gottman's lab, translated into a
clinical model (Gottman Method Couples Therapy) and a consumer curriculum
(The Seven Principles). The pieces the app uses:

- **The Four Horsemen.** Criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling, with
  named antidotes (gentle start-up, description of one's own need, culture of
  appreciation, taking responsibility, physiological self-soothing during a
  break).
- **Bids and turning toward.** Small requests for attention, and the partner's
  response (toward, away, against).
- **Repair attempts.** Any move that de-escalates a conflict in progress.
- **The Sound Relationship House.** Love maps, fondness and admiration, turning
  toward, positive perspective, managing conflict, making life dreams come true,
  creating shared meaning, with trust and commitment as the load-bearing walls.
- **Perpetual vs solvable problems.** The finding that a large share of couple
  disagreements are never resolved and must be managed rather than fixed.

### 2.2 What the research supports (Tier A / B)

- **Negative conflict behavior predicts relationship outcomes.** This is the
  durable, replicated core. Bradbury and Bodenmann's review treats hostile
  conflict and emotional disengagement as established markers of distress.
- **Contempt is treated across the literature as the most corrosive of the four.**
  Attribute it to Gottman's observational work rather than stating it as a settled
  causal law.
- **The Seven Principles curriculum shows real gains in an independent, non-US
  evaluation.** Zahl-Olsen, Thuen and Bertelsen, "The effectiveness of the
  in-person and online Gottman Seven Principles Couple Enhancement Program: A
  propensity score matching design", Journal of Marital and Family Therapy, 2024,
  50(4), 882 to 898. 490 program participants, 242 comparison participants in
  Norway, Revised Dyadic Adjustment Scale at baseline, post, and 6 months. Gains
  held at 6 months, and online delivery worked as well as in person. Read
  2026-07-19 at https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38961585/
  Caveat: this is propensity score matching, not randomization, so it is
  suggestive rather than causal.
  **This is the most directly relevant finding in the whole brief for Mend:
  a structured Gottman-derived curriculum delivered online performed as well as
  the same curriculum delivered in person, and facilitator clinical background did
  not change outcomes.**
- **Gottman Method for infidelity recovery has at least one randomized trial.**
  Irvine, Peluso, Benson, Cole, Cole, Gottman and Gottman, "A Pilot Study
  Examining the Effectiveness of Gottman Method Couples Therapy Over
  Treatment-as-Usual Approaches for Treating Couples Dealing with Infidelity",
  Journal of Family Psychotherapy, 2024. 49 couples randomized. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10664807231210123
  Caveat: pilot scale, and the model's developers are authors, which is a known
  source of inflated effects (see 2.4).

### 2.3 What the research does NOT support

**The divorce prediction accuracy figures.** The widely repeated "predicts divorce
with over 90 percent accuracy" claim comes from models fitted to the same sample
they were tested on. Heyman and Smith Slep, "The Hazards of Predicting Divorce
Without Crossvalidation", Journal of Marriage and Family, 2001, 63(2), 473 to 479,
built an equation that hit 90 percent overall accuracy in the development sample,
then applied it to a held-out crossvalidation sample from the same dataset. Overall
accuracy fell to 69 percent, sensitivity fell from 92 percent to 46 percent, and
the positive predictive value fell from 65 percent to 29 percent. Adjusting for the
real 16 percent base rate of divorce dropped positive predictive value to 21
percent. The authors concluded that dissemination of predictive power results in
popular media must wait for supportive data on independent samples. Read
2026-07-19 at https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1622921/

So: **Mend must never state or imply that any behavior, quiz, or pulse score
predicts whether a relationship will end.** Not in copy, not in a result screen,
not in marketing. This is both an evidence problem and a safety problem, because a
prediction of doom delivered by an app to a person in a fragile relationship is
harm.

**The 5:1 "magic ratio".** The ratio originates as a descriptive observation of
lab-observed interactions in stable vs unstable couples. There is no good evidence
that hitting a specific numeric ratio causes a good relationship, and the specific
number moved into the literature by repetition rather than by replication. See
Cook and colleagues, "The 'Magic' Positive-to-Negative Interaction Ratio:
Benefits, Applications, Cautions, and Recommendations", which concludes there is
no evidence that the 5:1 ratio, or any other specific ratio, has the effect
practitioners expect. Read 2026-07-19 at
https://www.researchgate.net/publication/323736594_The_Magic_Positive-to-Negative_Interaction_Ratio_Benefits_Applications_Cautions_and_Recommendations
The underlying idea, that stable relationships carry far more positive than
negative exchanges, is fine to teach. The number is not.

**The "86 percent vs 33 percent turning toward" figure.** This pair of numbers is
everywhere in secondary sources, all of which trace back to Gottman Institute
materials and trade books rather than to a clearly identifiable peer-reviewed
paper reporting those exact percentages. Searched 2026-07-19; no primary
peer-reviewed source located. **Do not print these numbers in the app.** Teach
turning toward as a practice, not as a statistic.

**The Sound Relationship House as a validated causal structure.** It is a
well-organized clinical teaching model. It has not been tested as a structural
model whose levels must be built in order. Present it as a map, never as a
sequence the research requires.

### 2.4 Method caveats that apply to all of the above

- Much of the Gottman outcome literature has the model's developers as authors.
  A systematic appraisal of allegiance effects in psychotherapy RCTs found larger
  effects when allegiance is present, with the effect strongest when the
  experimenter is also the treatment developer. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4570291/
- Observational lab findings describe correlations in behavior. They do not
  establish that changing the behavior changes the outcome. Section 6.3 covers
  the mediation problem, which is the sharpest version of this.

## 3. Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT / EFCT)

### 3.1 What it is

An attachment-based couple therapy. The central move is to slow a couple down out
of the surface fight and into the underlying attachment need. Core pieces:

- **The negative cycle.** The self-feeding pattern (commonly pursue and withdraw,
  or attack and defend) that both partners are stuck in. The cycle, not the
  partner, is named as the enemy.
- **Primary vs secondary emotion.** Anger, contempt and scorn are treated as
  secondary; the primary emotions underneath are typically fear, hurt, shame and
  inadequacy. Bradbury and Bodenmann describe EFCT as aiming "to facilitate
  expression of primary emotions ... rather than divisive secondary emotions ...
  thereby enhancing empathy and reducing attachment insecurity".
- **Attachment injuries.** A specific abandonment or betrayal at a critical moment
  of need, which blocks ordinary repair until it is addressed directly.
- **Blamer softening / bonding events.** The staged moment where the more critical
  partner expresses vulnerability and the other responds with care.

### 3.2 What the research supports (Tier A)

- **EFCT is one of the two best-supported couple therapies.** Bradbury and
  Bodenmann: meta-analyses support an approximate d of at least 0.80 for both
  behavioral couple therapy and EFCT, with 60 to 72 percent of couples showing
  reliable pre-post improvement in satisfaction. Read 2026-07-19 at the UCLA link
  above.
- **Dedicated meta-analysis.** Spengler, Lee, Wiebe and Wittenborn, "A
  Comprehensive Meta-Analysis on the Efficacy of Emotionally Focused Couple
  Therapy", Couple and Family Psychology: Research and Practice, 2024, 13(2),
  81 to 99. Reported effects: pretest to posttest d = 0.93; EFT versus other
  couple interventions d = 0.44; pretest to follow-up d = 0.86. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.ovid.com/journals/cfprp/fulltext/10.1037/cfp0000233~a-comprehensive-meta-analysis-on-the-efficacy-of-emotionally
- **Attachment injury resolution holds up over years.** Bradbury and Bodenmann
  report that EFCT's staged resolution of attachment injuries generates
  improvements in satisfaction, trust and forgiveness that persist over 3 years,
  citing Halchuk and colleagues 2010.

### 3.3 What the research does NOT support

- **EFT is not proven superior to behavioral approaches.** Bradbury and Bodenmann
  are explicit that comparisons between behavioral and emotion-focused models are
  "unwarranted because they have not been compared directly". The d = 0.44 for EFT
  versus other interventions comes from a meta-analysis conducted by EFT-affiliated
  researchers, not from head-to-head randomized trials against IBCT.
- **Allegiance risk is high.** EFT trials have historically been run by the
  developers and their trainees. Apply the same discount as in 2.4.
- **"Naming the cycle fixes the cycle" is not established.** See 6.3.

### 3.4 App translation

The cycle map is the single most portable EFT idea for a self-guided app: it
converts blame into a shared, external target. It is safe to present as a way of
seeing, not as a mechanism proven to change outcomes on its own. Attachment injury
work and blamer softening are therapist-led, emotionally risky processes. An app
should teach that they exist and route to a professional, not attempt to run them.

## 4. Integrative Behavioral Couple Therapy (IBCT)

### 4.1 What it is

Behavioral couple therapy plus an explicit acceptance component. The premise, in
Christensen's words as quoted by Bradbury and Bodenmann, is that IBCT "focuses more
on the emotional reactions of partners to the difficulties they encounter in their
relationships and less on the active solutions they can take to resolve these
difficulties, especially for what seem to be insoluble problems". Core moves:

- **Unified detachment.** Describing the problem as an "it" the couple observes
  together, rather than a fault in one person.
- **Empathic joining.** Expressing the soft feeling under the hard complaint, so
  the partner's reaction becomes understandable rather than offensive.
- **Tolerance building.** Reducing the sting of a behavior that is not going to
  change.
- **Change strategies.** Classic behavior exchange and communication training,
  used second rather than first.

### 4.2 What the research supports (Tier A)

The strongest long-term dataset in the field. Christensen, Atkins, Baucom and Yi,
"Marital status and satisfaction five years following a randomized clinical trial
comparing traditional versus integrative behavioral couple therapy", Journal of
Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 2010, 78(2), 225 to 235. 134 chronically and
seriously distressed married couples, roughly 8 months of therapy, followed every
6 months for 5 years. Read 2026-07-19 at https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20350033/

- Pre to post: d = 0.90 for IBCT, d = 0.71 for traditional behavioral couple
  therapy, not significantly different from each other.
- Five-year follow-up: d = 1.03 for IBCT, d = 0.92 for traditional.
- At 5 years: 50.0 percent of IBCT couples and 45.9 percent of traditional couples
  showed clinically significant improvement.
- At 5 years: 25.7 percent of IBCT couples and 27.9 percent of traditional couples
  were separated or divorced.
- IBCT was superior through 2 years, then the two treatments converged.

**These are the safest numbers in this entire brief to quote publicly**: a large
sample, a genuine randomized trial, a 5-year horizon, and honest reporting of the
couples who did not make it.

**The acceptance-first sequence has process support.** Bradbury and Bodenmann
report that observational coding across IBCT sessions found that couples who
responded well to treatment hit their lowest positivity and highest negativity
about midway through, while non-responders simply got steadily worse, citing
Sevier and colleagues 2015. Getting worse before getting better is a real
signature of the responding group, not a failure signal.

**Infidelity does not doom the outcome.** Marín, Christensen and Atkins,
"Infidelity and Behavioral Couple Therapy: Relationship Outcomes Over 5 Years
Following Therapy", Couple and Family Psychology: Research and Practice, 2014.
Infidelity couples who stayed together were indistinguishable in satisfaction from
non-infidelity couples. Read 2026-07-19 at
https://www.apa.org/pubs/journals/features/cfp-0000012.pdf
Caveat: 19 infidelity couples. Very small. Treat as encouraging, not as a
statistic to print.

### 4.3 What the research does NOT support

Acceptance vs change is not a proven superiority story. IBCT and traditional
behavioral couple therapy did not differ at termination on satisfaction, affective
communication, or problem-solving communication, in a large well-powered trial.
The app should present acceptance work as a real and underused option, not as the
better option.

### 4.4 App translation

IBCT translates unusually well to a self-guided product, because unified
detachment is fundamentally a reframing exercise that two people can do on paper.
Mend's existing "unfinished-fight autopsy" and rules-of-engagement work sit
naturally here. The acceptance framing is also the honest answer to perpetual
problems, and it protects the app from implying that every difference is fixable.

## 5. Attachment theory applied to adults

### 5.1 What holds up (Tier A)

- **Attachment is dimensional, not categorical.** People vary continuously along
  anxiety and avoidance. Taxometric analyses find the data fit a dimensional model
  rather than discrete types. Fraley and colleagues, "Are Adult Attachment Styles
  Categorical or Dimensional? A Taxometric Analysis of General and
  Relationship-Specific Attachment Orientations". Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.nathanwhudson.com/vita/pdf/Fraley%20et%20al.,%202015.pdf
- **Attachment orientation predicts relationship quality, modestly.** In the
  largest predictive study to date (see 6.1), attachment avoidance and attachment
  anxiety appear among the top five individual-difference predictors of
  relationship quality, but relationship-specific perceptions predict better.
- **Attachment is relationship-specific as well as general.** A person can be
  more secure in one relationship than another. This matters enormously for an
  app that might otherwise hand a user a fixed label.

### 5.2 What does NOT hold up (Tier C)

- **"You are an anxious type / avoidant type."** Types are a presentation
  convenience, not a finding. Sorting a user into one of four boxes and treating
  it as an identity misrepresents the science.
- **"Your childhood determined your attachment style, and therefore your
  relationship."** Fraley's own summary of the literature notes that associations
  between early caregiving and adult attachment are not strong and are not
  consistent across measures. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://scottbarrykaufman.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/Fraley-2019.pdf
  (Fraley, "Attachment in Adulthood: Recent Developments, Emerging Debates, and
  Future Directions", Annual Review of Psychology, 2019.)
- **"Anxious and avoidant partners are doomed together."** No credible source
  located for a specific pairing-outcome claim. Do not print pairing verdicts.

### 5.3 App translation

Mend's "lens quiz" is the pressure point. Rules:

1. Report positions on dimensions ("you lean toward needing more reassurance than
   your partner does right now"), never a type name as a verdict.
2. Frame results as about this relationship at this time, not about the person
   forever.
3. Never generate a compatibility verdict or a risk score from quiz results.
4. Say in plain language that these are self-report snapshots, not diagnoses.

## 6. What actually predicts relationship quality, and the mediation problem

### 6.1 The best available predictive evidence (Tier A)

Joel and colleagues, "Machine learning uncovers the most robust self-report
predictors of relationship quality across 43 longitudinal couples studies", PNAS,
2020. Random forests across 43 longitudinal dyadic datasets from 29 labs. Read
2026-07-19 at https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1917036117

- Top relationship-specific predictors: perceived partner commitment,
  appreciation, sexual satisfaction, perceived partner satisfaction, conflict.
- Top individual-difference predictors: life satisfaction, negative affect,
  depression, attachment avoidance, attachment anxiety.
- Relationship-specific variables predicted up to 45 percent of variance at
  baseline and up to 18 percent at study end. Individual differences predicted
  21 percent and 12 percent.
- The headline: the most powerful predictors are how a person perceives their own
  relationship, not stable traits of either partner.

**Product consequence.** The highest-value things an app can move are perceived
commitment and perceived appreciation. Both are directly addressable by a daily
two-person product: notes, daily questions, appreciation streaks, explicit
statements of commitment. This is the strongest evidence-based argument for
Mend's Space tab and appreciation weeks as core rather than decorative.

Also note: 55 to 88 percent of variance is not predicted by any of this. Humility
is the correct posture.

### 6.2 Two levers with direct experimental support

**Gratitude and appreciation.** Algoe's find-remind-bind theory holds that
gratitude functions to find, remind of, and bind to a responsive partner. A
5-week field experiment randomly assigning couples to a gratitude expression
technique increased the time couples spent co-present in everyday life relative to
control. Read 2026-07-19 at https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-15650-4
Caveat, from the same literature: effects on well-being were described as modest,
and effects depend on whether the expression is perceived as responsive rather
than performed. Read 2026-07-19 at
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5085264/

**Shared novel activities (self-expansion).** Aron's model predicts that novel,
exciting, challenging shared activity increases closeness and satisfaction, and
this has experimental support both in lab and in homework-style designs, including
a 4-week randomized online intervention involving about 1.5 hours of
participant-generated shared exciting activities that beat a waitlist control on
relationship quality. Read 2026-07-19 at
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11761567/

Both of these are cheap, low-risk, and app-shaped. They are the best justification
for Mend's games, challenges and shared experiences existing alongside the
conflict work.

### 6.3 The mediation problem, and why it matters more than anything else here

This is the finding most likely to be missed by a product team, and it should
change how Mend writes its content.

Bradbury and Bodenmann, on relationship education: changes in observed behavior
do not mediate treatment effects on satisfaction, citing Williamson and colleagues
2016, and observed communication may be as much a consequence of satisfaction as a
cause, citing Lavner and colleagues 2016. Their conclusion: "consistent, lasting,
and consequential changes in couple communication have not been documented".

In the US Army PREP for Strong Bonds randomized trial, intervention effects on
relationship quality were not apparent at the 2-year follow-up. Read 2026-07-19 at
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4237282/

**What this means for Mend.** The field's central assumption, that teaching
communication skills causes better relationships, is not established. Skills
content is still worth shipping, because programs containing it do produce
short-term gains, but the app must not claim a causal chain it cannot support.
Write "here is a way to try saying it" rather than "this technique will change your
relationship". Frame exercises as experiments the couple runs, not as fixes.

## 7. Relationship education programs: do they work, for whom, how much

### 7.1 The unselected-population answer: barely

- Hawkins and Erickson meta-analysis of relationship education for lower-income
  participants, reported in Bradbury and Bodenmann: overall d = 0.06 across 22
  tests, with satisfaction d = 0.07, communication d = 0.06, relationship
  aggression d = 0.06, and **relationship stability d = 0.00**. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25419911/ and the UCLA review PDF.
- Arnold and Beelmann, 16 tests: d = 0.11 on satisfaction, d = 0.12 on
  communication, which Bradbury and Bodenmann describe as treated couples
  performing better than roughly 5 percent of control couples.
- The largest federal trial: Supporting Healthy Marriage, N = 6,298 couples,
  randomized skills-based communication training, **mean d = 0.09** on satisfaction
  and communication and **no effect on relationship dissolution** despite a
  passive no-treatment control (Hsueh and colleagues 2012, as reported in Bradbury
  and Bodenmann). MDRC project page read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.mdrc.org/work/projects/supporting-healthy-marriage
- Building Strong Families, for unmarried low-income parents around a birth: the
  36-month report found no overall improvement in relationship quality or marriage
  rates. OPRE Report 2012-28B, November 2012, read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.mdrc.org/sites/default/files/bsf_36_mo_impact_exec_summ_0.pdf

Bradbury and Bodenmann's verdict on primary prevention is blunt: early optimism
that it would be easier to prevent relationship problems than treat them is now
doubted, "at least when standard forms of communication training are provided to
unselected couples".

### 7.2 The selected-population answer: meaningfully better

- Couples who are already struggling gain far more. Halford and colleagues 2015,
  as reported in Bradbury and Bodenmann: after an 18-hour workshop, couples
  initially low in satisfaction changed markedly more than couples initially high
  in satisfaction (**d = 0.44 versus d = 0.00**).
- Hawkins and Erickson report an effect-size difference of roughly d = 0.10
  favoring more distressed participants.
- A subset of empirically based programs reached d = 0.17 to 0.37 with low-income
  couples on various domains, versus the near-zero average.

**Product consequence.** Mend should be built for couples who know something is
wrong, not for generally happy couples looking for enrichment. The evidence says
the enrichment market is where effects go to zero. Mend's premise, a couple in
trouble walking a staged path, is the version of this that the research actually
supports.

### 7.3 Online delivery specifically: this is the good news

- **OurRelationship, RCT.** Doss and colleagues, "A randomized controlled trial of
  the web-based OurRelationship program", Journal of Consulting and Clinical
  Psychology, 2016, 84, 285 to 296. 300 couples with one or both partners
  distressed, roughly 8 hours of online program, waitlist control. Relationship
  satisfaction **d = 0.69**, relationship confidence d = 0.47, negative
  relationship quality d = 0.57. Individual functioning also improved: depressive
  symptoms d = 0.71, anxiety d = 0.94, perceived health d = 0.51, work functioning
  d = 0.57, quality of life d = 0.44. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26999504/
  As reported in Bradbury and Bodenmann: 57 percent recovered or improved,
  36 percent no change, **7 percent deteriorated**, and effects were stable across
  a 12-month follow-up.
- **Scaled to low-income couples.** Doss and colleagues 2020, Journal of Consulting
  and Clinical Psychology, 88(4), 283 to 294. 742 low-income couples (1,484
  individuals, median household income $27,000) randomized to OurRelationship,
  ePREP, or waitlist. Mean absolute d = 0.58 across mechanisms by program end.
  Differences between the two programs were minimal (median absolute d = 0.11).
  Read 2026-07-19 at https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32134290/
- **Online equals in person for a Gottman curriculum.** Zahl-Olsen and colleagues
  2024, cited in section 2.2.

**Product consequence.** A structured, guided online program for distressed
couples produces effects several times larger than generic relationship education,
and in the same range as some in-person work. That is the empirical case for Mend
existing. It is also a warning: the trials that produced d = 0.69 involved a
defined, completed 8-hour program with structure and, in some arms, coach contact.
Open-ended browsing is not the tested intervention. Completion of a defined
sequence is.

### 7.4 The attrition warning

Bradbury and Bodenmann note that in the relationship education literature
"attrition rates tend to be high, and long-term follow-ups are rare", and that a
core hope for technology-enabled interventions is reducing attrition in vulnerable
populations. In naturalistic couple therapy, about half of couples never finish.

Mend's product problem is therefore identical to the field's research problem:
getting two people to finish a sequence. Every design decision that increases
completion is an evidence-aligned decision.

## 8. Behavioral activation and habit formation

### 8.1 Behavioral activation

Behavioral activation is the principle that scheduling and doing valued activity
changes mood, rather than waiting to feel like it first. It has strong support as
a depression treatment.

- Meta-analytic effect versus controls: about -0.70 SD on depression scores across
  12 studies with experienced therapists. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448297/
- The COBRA trial found behavioral activation non-inferior to CBT for depression,
  delivered by junior and para-professional staff. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27461440/

**Relevance to Mend, stated carefully.** Behavioral activation is evidence-based
for depression, not for relationship repair. Its transferable idea is structural:
act first, feelings follow, and the acting can be scheduled by a non-expert
without losing effect. That is a defensible basis for Mend's micro-moves and
rituals. It is not a basis for claiming Mend treats depression. Depression is one
of the top individual-difference predictors of relationship quality (section 6.1),
which is a reason to surface professional help, not a reason to treat it in-app.

### 8.2 Habit formation

- Lally and colleagues, "How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the
  real world", European Journal of Social Psychology, 2010, 40, 998 to 1009. Median
  time to reach automaticity was 66 days, with a range from 18 to 254 days
  depending on behavior and person. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/ejsp.674 and secondary summary at
  https://www.cykelvaeksthuset.dk/media/az3linp0/promoting-habit-formation.pdf
  **Do not print "66 days to form a habit" as a rule.** Print the range, or print
  nothing. The median came from a small study and the spread is the finding.
- Implementation intentions (specifying when, where and how) have a long-standing
  meta-analytic reputation for medium-to-large effects on behavior initiation
  (Gollwitzer and Sheeran 2006), but more recent work reports much smaller effects
  in some domains, for example d = 0.14 for physical activity in Sheeran and
  colleagues 2024. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11920387/
  Teach the technique. Do not attach a number to it.

**Product consequence.** Mend's stage lengths (1 to 8 weeks) and its
ritual-repetition requirements are consistent with a habit-formation timescale of
weeks to months. The honest copy is "this takes weeks, and it varies a lot by
person", not a countdown to a promised transformation.

## 9. How this differs across relationship stages

The research base is thinnest exactly where the app most needs it, because most
strong trials recruited married couples. Everything below is stated with that
caveat.

### 9.1 Dating, not living together

- **The predictive core still applies.** Joel and colleagues' 43 datasets include
  dating samples; the top predictors (perceived commitment, appreciation, sexual
  satisfaction, perceived partner satisfaction, conflict) are not
  marriage-specific.
- **Commitment ambiguity is the distinctive risk.** Stanley, Rhoades and Markman's
  sliding vs deciding framework holds that transitions made by drifting rather
  than by explicit mutual decision carry more commitment risk. "Sliding Versus
  Deciding: Inertia and the Premarital Cohabitation Effect", Family Relations,
  2006. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1741-3729.2006.00418.x
- **Exit is cheap, which changes what "repair" means.** Constraints (shared lease,
  shared finances, shared children) are lower, so the relevant work is more about
  building dedication than dismantling barriers.
- **App implications.** Content should not assume cohabitation, shared money,
  shared children, or a legal frame. "Are we deciding or drifting" is a
  higher-value exercise here than a State of the Union ritual. Perpetual-problem
  and acceptance content is highly relevant, because dating couples are often
  deciding whether a difference is acceptable at all.

### 9.2 Living together, not married

- **The premarital cohabitation debate is unsettled and Mend should not take a
  side.** Rosenfeld and Roesler, Journal of Marriage and Family, 2019, 81, 42 to 58,
  found premarital cohabitation associated with lower odds of divorce in the first
  year of marriage but higher odds in later years. Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jomf.12530
  Manning, Smock and Kuperberg published a formal comment disputing it (JMF 2021,
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jomf.12724), and Rosenfeld and
  Roesler replied (JMF 2021,
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jomf.12744). All read 2026-07-19.
  **The app must not tell users that living together raises or lowers their divorce
  risk.** Two credible teams disagree in the same journal.
- **The useful, non-contested idea** is the commitment asymmetry: partners can hold
  different understandings of what living together means, and that mismatch is
  itself the problem worth surfacing.
- **App implications.** Explicit commitment conversations, money conversations, and
  "what does this mean to each of us" exercises are the stage-appropriate work.
  Avoid marriage-framed language entirely.

### 9.3 Married or in a formalized long-term partnership

- **This is where the strongest evidence sits** (sections 1 through 4), and where
  the IBCT 5-year numbers apply directly.
- **Constraints are high**, so distress can persist for years without dissolution.
  That means the relevant outcome is quality, not survival.
- **App implications.** Perpetual problems, the acceptance frame, rituals of
  connection, and long-horizon expectations are all well-matched here. This is the
  only stage where the app can honestly point to 5-year outcome data.

### 9.4 Reconciling, or repairing after a breach

- **The good news is real but the numbers are not.** Web searches on 2026-07-19 for
  reconciliation rates and "marriages that survive infidelity" returned almost
  exclusively content-farm statistics pages with uncited or fabricated figures.
  **None of those numbers may enter the app.** No credible source for a
  general reconciliation success rate was located.
- **What is citable:** Marín, Christensen and Atkins 2014 found that among couples
  who went through behavioral couple therapy, infidelity couples who stayed
  together were indistinguishable in satisfaction from non-infidelity couples over
  5 years (19 infidelity couples; small sample). Read 2026-07-19 at
  https://www.apa.org/pubs/journals/features/cfp-0000012.pdf
- **EFT's attachment injury resolution** produces gains in satisfaction, trust and
  forgiveness that persisted over 3 years (Halchuk and colleagues 2010, as reported
  in Bradbury and Bodenmann).
- **Gottman Method for infidelity** has one small randomized pilot (section 2.2).
- **App implications.** Reconciliation content should be sequenced: safety and
  transparency first, then the injured partner's account being fully heard, then
  the other partner's remorse and changed behavior, then rebuilding. It should
  carry the strongest professional-referral prompts in the product, because this is
  the highest-risk track and the evidence for self-guided work here is weakest.
  Never imply a probability of success.

### 9.5 Same-sex and LGBTQ+ couples

- The predictors of relationship quality are broadly the same. Kurdek's
  longitudinal program concluded that same-sex relationships operate on
  essentially the same principles as different-sex relationships, while finding
  differences in autonomy, barriers to leaving, and dissolution rates. Read
  2026-07-19 at https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.0963-7214.2005.00375.x
- **App implications.** The core content generalizes and should be written in
  gender-neutral language by default. Do not build gendered conflict content
  (for example "men withdraw, women pursue") as if it were a law; pursue and
  withdraw is a role pattern, not a gender fact.

### 9.6 A stage-difference caution

Bradbury and Bodenmann note that prevention samples now vary so much across
sexual orientation, commitment, relationship stage, status and quality that
"effect sizes in CRE meta-analyses may be losing their diagnostic value", and that
frameworks for capturing this diversity "have yet to emerge". Mend should treat
stage as a content-routing variable, not as a claim generator.

## 10. The do-not-say list

Never state these as fact anywhere in the product, marketing, App Store copy, or
notifications.

| Claim | Why not |
| --- | --- |
| "Predicts divorce with 90+ percent accuracy" | Accuracy collapses on crossvalidation; positive predictive value fell from 65 percent to 29 percent, and to 21 percent at the real base rate (Heyman and Smith Slep 2001) |
| Any prediction that a specific user's relationship will end | Same reason, plus direct harm risk |
| "You need 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative" | No evidence any specific ratio has the claimed effect |
| "Happy couples turn toward bids 86 percent of the time, divorcing couples 33 percent" | No primary peer-reviewed source located |
| "Your love language is X, and speaking it will improve your relationship" | Impett, Park and Muise, Current Directions in Psychological Science, 2024, 33(1), 23 to 31: no strong support for a single preferred language, for exactly five languages, or for matching improving satisfaction. People appreciate all five. Read 2026-07-19 at https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09637214231217663 |
| "You are an anxious/avoidant/secure type" | Attachment is dimensional, relationship-specific, and changeable |
| "Your childhood attachment determines your relationship" | Associations exist but are not strong or measurement-consistent (Fraley 2019) |
| "Living together before marriage raises (or lowers) your divorce risk" | Two credible teams actively disagree in the same journal |
| "X percent of marriages survive infidelity" / "X percent of separated couples reconcile" | Only content-farm sources found; no credible primary source |
| "It takes 21 days (or 66 days) to build a habit" | Median 66 days with a range of 18 to 254 in a small study; the spread is the finding |
| "Better communication causes a better relationship" | Observed behavior change has not been shown to mediate satisfaction gains; lasting communication change has not been documented |
| "This app will save your relationship" | Even full-length professional couple therapy leaves 21 percent unchanged and 13 percent worse at termination |
| Any claim implying couple work is appropriate where there is coercive control or violence | Contradicts the safety posture and can escalate danger |

Also avoid, as a category: **any score, percentile, or grade that a user could
read as a verdict on whether their relationship will last.** Pulse checks are for
routing to the right content and to professional help, not for prophecy.

## 11. Safety, restated in research terms

The safety posture is non-negotiable and this research strengthens it rather than
softening it.

- **Screening is not optional.** Intimate partner violence is highly prevalent
  among relationship-therapy-seeking populations, with reported rates as high as
  74 percent in some samples, and universal screening is recommended yet
  inconsistently practiced. Todahl and Walters, "Universal Screening for Intimate
  Partner Violence: A Systematic Review", Journal of Marital and Family Therapy,
  2011. Read 2026-07-19 at https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21745237/
  Mend's onboarding gate is doing the job that clinicians are documented to skip.
- **Conjoint work can be contraindicated.** Where there is coercive control, joint
  exercises that require honest disclosure can expose a partner to retaliation.
  The gate, the per-track red flags, and the crisis resources stay free, stay
  unpaywalled, and stay reachable from every screen.
- **Deterioration is a measured outcome.** 13 percent of couples deteriorated by
  termination in the large IBCT trial, and 7 percent deteriorated in the
  OurRelationship trial. An app that never offers an exit ramp is not matching the
  evidence.
- **"Educational tool, not therapy" is accurate, not defensive.** The tested
  online programs that produced d = 0.69 were structured programs, several with
  human coach contact, delivered to couples who had consented to a research
  protocol. Mend is not those trials and should not borrow their numbers as
  promises.

## 12. Decision summary for the product

1. Build for couples who already know something is wrong. Effects for
   general-population enrichment are near zero (d = 0.06 to 0.11); effects for
   already-distressed couples are several times larger (d = 0.44 versus d = 0.00 in
   one direct within-study comparison).
2. Make completing a defined sequence the product's core loop. The online trials
   that worked tested finite structured programs, not libraries.
3. Prioritize perceived commitment and perceived appreciation. They are the top
   two relationship-specific predictors of relationship quality across 43
   longitudinal datasets, and they are exactly what a shared two-person daily
   surface can move.
4. Keep the play, novelty and shared-experience content. Self-expansion has direct
   experimental support and is not filler.
5. Teach communication skills without claiming they cause the outcome. Frame every
   exercise as an experiment, not a fix.
6. Teach acceptance alongside change. It is the honest answer to perpetual
   problems and it is backed by the field's best long-term trial.
7. Never predict, score, or grade the future of a relationship.
8. Write everything in relationship-neutral language, and route by stage: dating
   (deciding vs drifting), cohabiting (commitment asymmetry), married
   (perpetual problems and rituals), reconciling (sequenced repair plus the
   strongest referral prompts).
9. Expect and design for attrition. It is the field's biggest unsolved problem and
   the one a good app is uniquely positioned to beat.
10. Attribute everything. Gottman, EFT, IBCT and PREP are named traditions with
    real literatures and real limits, and saying so is what makes the app
    trustworthy.

## 14. Known gaps in this brief

Stated plainly so nobody treats absence of evidence as evidence.

- No credible general reconciliation-rate or infidelity-survival statistic was
  located. Treat as unknown.
- No located research on app-delivered couple work for dating (non-cohabiting)
  couples specifically. The OurRelationship trials skew toward married and
  cohabiting samples.
- No located research on relationship apps used asymmetrically, where one partner
  engages and the other does not. This is likely Mend's most common real-world
  scenario and it is scientifically uncharted.
- The 86/33 turning-toward figure, and several other widely circulated Gottman
  numbers, could not be traced to primary peer-reviewed sources within this
  search. Absence of a located source is not proof they are wrong, but it is
  sufficient reason not to print them.
- Effect sizes throughout come from samples that skew white, different-sex,
  and North American or Western European.
